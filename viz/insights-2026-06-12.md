# llm-token-harness — Hardened Run Insights (June 12, 2026)

**Run:** 23-task hardened dataset, 12 configs, single run. North-star: **CPC = run cost / tasks solved.**
**Caveat up front:** n=23, one run, no variance. Treat config-vs-config gaps of 1–2 tasks as noise; treat the structural patterns (which *axes* break models, which *distractor* gets picked) as the real signal. The June-10 grid was a different 15-task dataset and is **not** directly comparable score-for-score — it's only useful for "did the spread widen."

---

## 1. Executive summary

- **Hardening worked.** Score spread went from 4 points (June-10: everyone 10–14/15) to **10 points** (June-12: 9–19/23). The ceiling moved up and the floor dropped — the dataset now discriminates.
- **CPC ≠ accuracy, and that's the whole point.** The two top-accuracy tiers (18–19/23) cost between **$0.0079 and $0.044 per correct task** depending on config — a **~5.6× CPC spread at near-identical quality.** $/token would have hidden this entirely.
- **GPT-5.4 nano is the efficiency story.** 16/23 at **$0.000476 CPC** — it *strictly dominates* Opus 4.8 (also 16/23, **$0.025654 CPC**, ~54× more expensive per correct task). A nano beating a flagship on both axes is the headline.
- **Parallel invocation is the single sharpest discriminator.** Exactly **1 of 12** configs solved `halverson_dispute_02`; nano fired 4 distinct searches while 8 of 11 others collapsed to two `list_documents` calls. This task alone separates "knows when to fan out" from everyone else.
- **Near-miss distractors now bite — and only one of them.** Every distractor pick across the whole run was `summarize_document`; `search_history` drew **zero**. Sonnet 4.6 is the heaviest picker (5 tasks, 11 total invocations) and loses tasks because of it.
- **Fable 5's effort knob now buys tokens, not correctness.** low/med/high = 19/19/18 score on 2586/3144/3800 output tokens. Higher effort *regressed* one task. On the data, "high" is a strict CPC loss.

### The Pareto frontier (maximize score, minimize CPC)

Ignoring gemma (free but slowest by 6× and lowest accuracy — a separate "local/zero-marginal-cost" regime), the non-dominated configs are:

1. **gpt-4o-mini** — cheapest ($0.000402 CPC), anchors the low end at 13/23.
2. **gpt-5.4-nano** — $0.000476 CPC, 16/23. Only ~18% more CPC than 4o-mini for +3 tasks.
3. **gpt-5.4-mini** — $0.001621 CPC, 17/23. +1 task over nano at ~3.4× CPC.
4. **Fable 5 (low)** — $0.038652 CPC, **19/23** (the top score). The *only* way to buy the 18th and 19th correct task, at a steep premium.

Everything else is **dominated**: some frontier config matches or beats its score at lower CPC.

- **gpt-5.5** (18/23, $0.012619) is dominated by **opus-4-6**? No — opus-4-6 is *more* expensive. gpt-5.5 is dominated by Fable-low on score (19>18) but Fable is pricier; gpt-5.5 is *not* strictly dominated on the cost axis among the 18s. The cleaner read: **gpt-5.5 is the cheapest 18/23** ($0.012619 CPC), making it a soft frontier point. Opus-4-6 (18/23, $0.020064) is **strictly dominated by gpt-5.5** (same score, ~1.6× cheaper).
- **claude-opus-4-8** (16/23, $0.025654) is **strictly dominated by gpt-5.4-nano** (same score, 54× cheaper) and by gpt-5.4-mini (higher score, 16× cheaper). This is the starkest domination in the grid.
- **claude-sonnet-4-6** (17/23, $0.012507) ties gpt-5.4-mini on score but costs **7.7× more per correct**. Dominated.
- **claude-haiku-4-5** (15/23, $0.004653) is beaten on score by nano (16) at **~10× lower CPC**. Dominated.

### "$/solved-task vs $/token," made concrete

The 18–19/23 tier is where the debate lives. Four configs land there:

| Config | Score | CPC ($/solved) | Output tok |
|---|---|---|---|
| gpt-5.5 | 18 | $0.012619 | 2542 |
| claude-opus-4-6 | 18 | $0.020064 | 4062 |
| Fable 5 (high) | 18 | $0.044171 | 3800 |
| Fable 5 (low) | **19** | $0.038652 | 2586 |

A buyer optimizing **$/token** would rank by output volume and might pick gpt-5.5 or pay up for a flagship's tokens. A buyer optimizing **CPC** sees that **gpt-5.5 delivers 18 correct tasks for 3.5× less per-correct than Fable-low's 19**, and that the marginal cost of Fable's *one extra* correct task is enormous. CPC turns "which is cheaper per token" into the decision that actually matters: **how many dollars to get the job done right.** That is the argument the blog is making, and the nano-vs-Opus-4.8 row is the cleanest single proof of it.

## 3. What discriminates models now — ranking the axes

Using `task_difficulty` (pass count across 12 configs), the hardest tasks cluster by axis. Lower pass count = sharper discriminator.

| Task | n_pass/12 | Axis |
|---|---|---|
| `halverson_dispute_02` | **1** | **Parallel invocation** |
| `vendor_renewal_decompose_01` | **2** | Query decompose |
| `vendor_autorenew_02` | **3** | Long chain / constraint filter |
| `easton_amendment_02` | 4 (alt) | Chain, non-adjacent state |
| `halverson_dispute_01` | 4 | Long-chain entry |
| `halverson_dispute_05` | 5 (alt) | Long chain |
| `vendor_autorenew_04` | 5 | Chain / filter |
| (baseline floor + easy chains) | 8–12 | — |

**Ranked by spread created:**

1. **Parallel invocation (axis 4) — by far the sharpest.** `halverson_dispute_02`: 1/12 pass. The failure mode is *uniform and diagnostic*: 8 configs collapsed the task into two `list_documents` calls (Fable all 3 efforts, opus-4-8, gpt-5.4-mini, sonnet, gpt-5.5, opus-4-6 partially). Only **gpt-5.4-nano** fanned out into 4 distinct `search` calls and matched both required specs. gemma emitted *no* calls. This isn't a near-miss — it's a clean capability line.

2. **Query decompose — second sharpest, and possibly a grading artifact (see §6).** `vendor_renewal_decompose_01`: 2/12 pass — and *only Sonnet 4.6 and Opus 4.6* got it. Note nano, gpt-5.5, all Fable efforts, and opus-4-8 all *failed* it. When the only two passers are an adjacent-generation Anthropic pair and the newer, nominally-stronger models miss it, suspect the expected answer is narrowly specified rather than the task being "hard." Flag for adjudication.

3. **Long chains with non-adjacent state (axis 1).** The `halverson_dispute` 5-step chain is the graveyard: gpt-4o-mini and gemma went **0/5**; the best (nano, gpt-5.4-mini, gpt-5.5, opus-4-6, all Fable) max out at **3/5**. *No config solved the full Halverson chain.* `vendor_autorenew` (4 steps) similarly tops out — only Fable low/med hit 4/4. Chains spread the field but more gradually than parallel.

4. **Constraint-dense filters (axis 2).** Hardest to isolate because filters are embedded in chain steps (`vendor_autorenew_02/04`, 3 and 5 passes). They contribute to the chain attrition above rather than standing alone. Moderate discriminator.

The **baseline floor held**: 6 of the easy floor/early-chain tasks scored 12/12 (`corvid_fetch_01`, `nda_inventory_01`, `q1_2025_inventory_01`, `nonsolicit_search_01`, `termination_topk_01`, `easton_amendment_01/03`). Single-turn selection from a small tool set remains saturated — exactly the prior finding, now confirmed as the *floor* while the hard axes do the work.

## 4. Behavioral findings

### Distractor susceptibility — it's all `summarize_document`

- **Only 3 of 12 configs picked any distractor**, and **100% of picks were `summarize_document`.** `search_history` (the other near-miss) drew **zero** picks; the 3 categorical distractors (web_search, tag_document, create_alert) drew zero. Prior run: zero distractor picks total. So the near-miss design *worked* — but only one of the two near-misses is actually tempting.
- **Sonnet 4.6 is the problem child:** picks on 5 distinct tasks (halverson_dispute_03/04/05, vendor_autorenew_02, stonebridge_term_01) totaling **11 invocations**. And it *fails* the tasks it reaches for it on: it scores 0 on halverson_dispute_03 and 04 (where the corpus says it summarized instead of fetching) and 0 on vendor_autorenew_02 and stonebridge_term_01. Its 17/23 would plausibly be higher without this reflex.
- **Why `summarize_document` specifically:** it shadows `get_document` on tasks that ask for *content of a known doc*. A model that reasons "the user wants the substance, summarize gives substance" picks it — it's the *semantically closest wrong answer*, which is exactly what it was designed to be. `search_history` shadows `search` but no task framed the need as "what did we look at before," so it never activated. Lesson for V2: the near-miss only discriminates if a task actively baits it.
- gpt-4o-mini (2 tasks) and gemma (1 task) also dipped into `summarize_document`; the GPT-5.x line, opus, and Fable were clean.

### Decompose vs. parallel — a behavioral split

The data hints at two different "break complex query into pieces" instincts:
- The **parallel** task (`halverson_dispute_02`) wants *multiple simultaneous `search` calls*. Only nano did it.
- The **decompose** task (`vendor_renewal_decompose_01`) wants the explicit `query_decompose` tool. Only the Anthropic 4.6 pair did it.
- **No config did both.** nano solved parallel but failed decompose; Sonnet/Opus-4.6 solved decompose but failed parallel. This is suggestive (each is n=1) but interesting: "fan out with raw searches" and "call the decompose tool" appear to be *different reflexes*, not a single planning competence.

### Effort-knob economics (Fable 5)

| Effort | Score | Output tok | Total cost | CPC |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| low | **19** | 2586 | $0.73438 | $0.038652 |
| medium | **19** | 3144 | $0.76228 | $0.040120 |
| high | 18 | 3800 | $0.79508 | $0.044171 |

The knob now **scales cost monotonically (output tokens +47% low→high) but not quality.** low=medium on score; high *regressed* one task (lost `vendor_autorenew_04`, kept everything else). On June-10 the knob was a pure no-op (low=med=high=10). So it went from "does nothing" to "spends more for the same-or-worse result." On this dataset, **Fable 5 (low) strictly dominates medium and high** — there is no reason to turn the knob up. Worth flagging to whoever owns Fable's pricing/effort story.

## 5. Surprises / counterintuitive results

- **A nano beats two flagships on both axes.** gpt-5.4-nano (16/23, $0.000476) matches Opus 4.8's *score* at **1/54th** the CPC, and beats Haiku 4.5 (15/23) at ~1/10th. Pricier ≠ better here.
- **Opus 4.8 < Opus 4.6** on this run (16 vs 18), and it's the *most expensive non-Fable config* ($0.41 total). The newer, dearer flagship underperformed its predecessor by 2 tasks. n is small, but on a benchmark designed to reward reasoning this is a genuine eyebrow-raiser — Opus 4.8 also uniquely failed the parallel task with the lazy two-`list_documents` collapse.
- **High effort made Fable worse.** Spending more reasoning budget cost a task. Counter to the "more thinking = better" prior.
- **gpt-5.5 (18/23) outscores both Opus configs** while costing less than either. The mid-tier OpenAI model is punching above the Anthropic flagships on this eval.
- **The decompose task's passer list is inverted from expectation:** only the *older* 4.6 Anthropic models solve it; every newer/stronger model misses. Either decompose is a fading behavior or (more likely) the grader is narrow — see §6.

## 6. Caveats & threats to validity

- **n = 23, single run, no variance/error bars.** Any 1–2 task config gap is within plausible run-to-run noise. **Do not** report "model A beats model B by 1 task" as a finding. The *structural* results (parallel = 1/12, all distractor picks = summarize_document) are robust because they're categorical and repeated across configs.
- **All-or-nothing scoring** means one wrong arg = 0. This inflates the apparent difficulty of constraint-dense and decompose tasks and can manufacture "capability gaps" that are really arg-format mismatches. The **decompose task (2/12, only Anthropic 4.6)** is the prime suspect for a grading artifact — investigate the expected spec before publishing it as a capability claim.
- **Simulated corpus.** Tool responses are hardcoded JSON. We measure *retrieval reasoning*, not retrieval quality. A model that picks the right tool with the right args against a fake corpus may still flounder on real noisy results. The blog should not claim end-to-end agent quality.
- **Single-turn framing.** Chains build real history but each step is still scored as one prompt→one call. Real multi-turn error compounding (a wrong step-2 poisoning step-3) is *partially* captured via history but the scoring is per-step independent, so it understates cascade failure.
- **Reasoning-token capture is incomplete.** Only gpt-5.5 (822) and gemma (2225) report `reasoning_tokens`; everything else is 0 — including all Fable efforts, where we *know* effort scales internal work (output tokens prove it) but reasoning tokens aren't surfaced. **CPC for thinking models is therefore an under- or mis-count of true compute**, and Fable's effort economics are inferred from output tokens, not measured reasoning. Be explicit about this in any cost claim.
- **gemma is a different regime.** $0 marginal cost makes its CPC $0 and uninformative for the dollar story; its 28.5s latency (6× the next slowest) and 9/23 floor matter for the latency axis only. Don't put it on the same CPC chart without an asterisk.
- **Cross-run comparison is loose.** June-10 was 15 different tasks. "Spread widened 4→10" is sound (it's about *dispersion*, robust to task identity); per-model score deltas across runs are **not** comparable.

## 7. Blog-angle suggestions

1. **"Cost per correct, not cost per token: how a $0.0005 nano beat a $0.026 flagship."** Lead with the gpt-5.4-nano vs Opus-4.8 row — same score, 54× CPC gap. It's the single cleanest number in the dataset and it *is* the $/solved-task argument. Honest: same score, so it's a pure efficiency win, not a quality claim.

2. **"The knob that spends but doesn't deliver: Fable 5's effort dial in numbers."** low/med/high = 19/19/18 on rising cost; high regressed. A concrete, slightly contrarian take on reasoning-effort controls — buy the cheapest effort tier until proven otherwise. (Caveat the reasoning-token gap so it's not overclaimed.)

3. **"What actually separates frontier models now: parallel tool-calling."** 1/12 on the parallel task; the uniform "collapse to two list_documents" failure is a vivid, reproducible story about a *specific* missing competence — and nano being the lone solver subverts the "bigger model wins" expectation. The strongest *capability* (not just cost) narrative in the run.

A fourth, softer angle if wanted: **"Two ways to plan, and no model does both"** — the decompose-vs-parallel split — but only run it if §6's grading-artifact suspicion on the decompose task clears adjudication.
